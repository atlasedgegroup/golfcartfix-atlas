DS and Precedent diagnostic guides. Covers the On-Board Computer (OBC) system that trips up most 48V Club Car owners, plus solenoid, controller, and charger procedures.

Club Car Model Overview

Club Car produces two main platforms: the DS (1981–present, the most common model in the field) and the Precedent (2004–present, the newer design with independent suspension). Both are available in gas and electric versions, but the electric platforms differ significantly by voltage generation.

DS Electric (36V and 48V)

Pre-1996 DS carts run a 36V system (six 6V batteries). From 1996 onward, Club Car moved DS to 48V (six 8V batteries). The critical diagnostic distinction is the OBC: 1994.5–2014 DS models with 48V have an On-Board Computer that manages charging and prevents the charger from activating if pack voltage drops too low. This is the number-one source of Club Car charging confusion.

Precedent Electric (48V)

All Precedent electric models run 48V. Pre-2014 models have the OBC; 2014+ models dropped it. The Precedent uses an IQ or PDS controller system. The PDS (Precision Drive System) found on 2004–2011 Precedent and some DS models uses a Hall-effect accelerator sensor rather than a traditional potentiometer.

The OBC: Know It Before You Diagnose Anything

The OBC (On-Board Computer) is a small computer module that communicates with the charger and monitors battery pack voltage. If the pack voltage drops below approximately 34V (total 48V pack), the OBC signals the charger to shut off, preventing a charge cycle from starting. The result: you plug in the charger, nothing happens, and you assume the charger or batteries are failed. Often it's just the OBC protecting a deeply discharged pack. The fix is either a manual bypass or individual battery trickle charging to bring the pack above the OBC threshold.
