Club Car Charger Not Charging: Diagnosis Guide

Covers Lester Summit, DPI, and older chargers. OBC-related no-charge is the most common cause on 48V models — this guide walks through it and the actual charger/battery scenarios.

Fast Answer

On 48V Club Cars (1994.5–2014): plug in charger, does the charger make any sound or show any indicator light? Complete silence = OBC blocking the charge (see OBC bypass guide). Charger clicks on then off immediately = pack voltage too low for charger to complete handshake. Charger hums but current doesn't flow = check DC output cable and charger receptacle connection.

Complete Silence

OBC blocking signal. Disconnect OBC 6-pin connector and retry. See OBC bypass guide.

Clicks On/Off

Pack too low for charger handshake. Manually trickle-charge each battery to bring pack above 34V threshold.

Runs But No Current

Check DC output cable at both ends. Also verify charger receptacle on cart isn't corroded.

Systematic Diagnosis

Step 1: Identify Your Charger Type

Club Car used several charger types over the years. Lester Summit is the most common OEM unit on 48V models (2000s–2014). Older carts used a Lestronic II. Some fleet carts use DPI units. The Lester Summit and Lestronic both communicate with the OBC; aftermarket chargers like Schauer or MODZ Smart may bypass the OBC entirely.

Step 2: Check Pack Voltage

Measure total pack voltage at the main battery posts. Below 34V total = the charger won't engage (OBC protection). Between 34–40V = low but above threshold, charger should start. Above 50V = pack is already near full, charger may shut off quickly (this is normal).

Step 3: OBC Bypass Test

If voltage is below 34V and the charger won't start: disconnect the OBC 6-pin connector (under the seat) and retry the charger. If it now starts charging, the OBC was blocking it due to low voltage. Charge the pack fully with OBC disconnected, then reconnect the OBC. See the full OBC bypass guide.

Step 4: Check the Charger Receptacle

The charging receptacle on the cart (the port you plug the charger cable into) is a common failure point. Inspect the pins for corrosion, burning, or bent contacts. A bad receptacle prevents the charger from making proper electrical contact. Receptacle replacement: $15–$40.

Step 5: Test the Charger Output

If the pack voltage is in range and the OBC is ruled out, test the charger's DC output. Set multimeter to DC voltage. With the charger unplugged from the cart, plug it into AC and measure the output pins. A working charger should show the pack voltage or slightly higher (48–58V). Zero volts output = failed charger.
