Club Car OBC Bypass: 5-Minute Procedure

The On-Board Computer on 1994.5–2014 Club Car 48V prevents charging when pack voltage is too low. This bypass lets you charge a deeply discharged pack without replacing the OBC.

Fast Answer: Which Models Have an OBC?

Affected models: Club Car DS 48V (1994.5–2014) and Precedent 48V (2004–2013). The 2014+ Precedent and all 36V models do not have an OBC. If your charger plugs in and does absolutely nothing on a 48V model, and your pack is deeply discharged, the OBC is likely blocking the charge cycle.

Temporary Bypass

Unplug the OBC 6-pin connector during charging only. Reconnect after charge is complete. No wiring changes.

Permanent Bypass

Run a jumper wire from battery B+ to the OBC signal wire. Charger always sees full voltage. OBC is bypassed permanently.

OBC Replacement

If OBC itself is failed (not just low voltage issue), replace for $35–$80. Often the better long-term fix.

Why the OBC Blocks Charging

The Club Car OBC (On-Board Computer) monitors the total battery pack voltage via a signal wire. When the charger is plugged in, the OBC communicates with the charger and tells it whether to begin a charge cycle. If pack voltage is below approximately 34V (a deeply discharged 48V pack), the OBC signals the charger to stay off — the logic being that a severely discharged pack may have a failed cell that shouldn't be force-charged.

The problem: once a pack gets below that threshold through normal discharge or sitting unused, you can't charge it back up through normal means. The bypass breaks the OBC's hold on the charger so you can recover the pack.

⚠ Important: The OBC bypass is a recovery procedure for deeply discharged packs. If you permanently bypass the OBC, your charger will no longer have the low-voltage protection the OBC provides. Individual battery voltage testing becomes more important to catch failed cells before they damage the charger.

Temporary Bypass Procedure (Recommended First Step)

  1. Locate the OBC. Lift the Club Car seat. The OBC is a small black rectangular module, typically mounted on the driver's side of the battery tray or on the frame rail. It has a 6-pin gray or black connector.
  2. Disconnect the 6-pin OBC connector. This disconnects the OBC's communication line from the charger circuit. With the OBC signal removed, most Club Car chargers default to charging mode.
  3. Plug in your charger. The charger should now attempt a charge cycle without the OBC blocking it. Let it run until complete.
  4. Reconnect the OBC connector after charging. The OBC is now restored and will resume its normal protective monitoring role.
  5. Test individual batteries. If the pack was deeply discharged, test each battery individually after the charge cycle to identify any failed cells.

Permanent Bypass Wiring

For a permanent bypass: on the OBC, find the B+ signal wire in the 6-pin connector (typically orange in Club Car wiring). Connect this wire directly to a fused connection on the positive battery terminal. This tells the charger it always sees full pack voltage and will always allow a charge cycle. Use a 5A inline fuse on the jumper wire.

Many Club Car owners doing a charger upgrade (replacing the original Lester charger with a DPI or Schauer unit) do a permanent OBC bypass as part of the installation, since newer smart chargers have their own battery protection logic.

When to Replace the OBC Instead

If the bypass procedure lets you charge but the OBC connector shows corrosion, bent pins, or burn marks — or if the cart had a previous no-charge problem that wasn't caused by a low pack — the OBC itself may be failed and should be replaced. A new OBC is $35–$80 and installs in 20 minutes.
