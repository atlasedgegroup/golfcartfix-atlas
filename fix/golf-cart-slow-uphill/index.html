Golf Cart Slow on Hills: Battery Sag to Motor Wear

Loss of hill-climbing power has five main causes: battery voltage sag, cable resistance, motor brush wear, controller current limiting, and mechanical drivetrain drag. Diagnose in that order.

Fast Answer: Voltage Under Load

The single most diagnostic test for slow-on-hills: measure battery pack voltage while the cart is climbing a hill (or under heavy load). Have a helper drive while you probe the pack. A healthy 48V pack should stay above 44V under load. If it drops below 40V, you have a battery problem — either the pack is at end of life or one cell is failing catastrophically under current draw. Stop there before checking anything else.

Voltage Sag Test

Measure pack voltage during hill climb. Drop below 44V (48V pack) = battery problem first.

Cable Resistance

Corroded or undersized battery cables cause voltage drop. Inspect and clean all connection points.

Motor Brushes (DC)

Worn brushes = reduced current transfer = reduced torque. Inspect brush length on DC motor carts.

Controller Limiting

Some controllers have adjustable current limits. A previous reset or programming change may have reduced max current.

Step 1: Battery Voltage Sag

Lead-acid batteries have internal resistance that increases as they age. Under heavy current draw (climbing a hill), high internal resistance causes the terminal voltage to drop significantly. A pack that reads 50V at rest but drops to 38V under load is failing. Do the load test described above. If voltage sags more than 6–8V under normal hill load, the batteries are the primary problem.

Individual Battery Load Test

Test each battery separately under load to find the weak cell. Drive 50 feet (or apply load with headlights and accessories), stop, and immediately measure each battery. A good cell recovers quickly; a failing cell stays depressed for 30+ seconds. The battery that stays lowest is the one failing.

Step 2: Battery Cable Connections

Corroded battery terminals or loose cable connections add resistance to the main power circuit. This reduces the voltage that reaches the controller and motor, exactly mimicking weak batteries. Inspect all battery terminals for white or green corrosion. Clean with a terminal brush and baking soda solution. Check the torque on all battery terminal bolts — loose connections are common after vibration over time. Also check the cable lugs where they connect to the solenoid and controller.

Step 3: Motor Brush Wear (DC Motor Carts)

DC series-wound motors (used in EZGO TXT, Club Car DS/Precedent pre-2014, Yamaha Drive pre-Drive2, and G-series) have carbon brushes that transfer current to the spinning armature. When brushes wear down to under 1/4”, contact becomes intermittent. You'll notice reduced power, especially under load, and sometimes a burning smell or sparks visible through the brush covers. Inspect by removing the plastic brush access covers on the motor end. Replace brushes in sets ($30–$60).

Step 4: Controller Current Limit

Programmable controllers (Alltrax, Curtis 1206, Lester Summit) have adjustable maximum current settings. If a previous owner or shop reset the controller to a conservative profile, max torque may be limited. If you have a programmable controller and the batteries and motor check out, connecting a programming cable and verifying the current limit setting is worth doing ($0 if you have the cable, $50–$100 if a shop does it).

Step 5: Mechanical Drag

Dragging brakes, seized wheel bearings, or binding differential all increase rolling resistance and reduce effective hill-climbing ability. Jack up each rear wheel and spin by hand. Should spin freely with minimal resistance. Dragging = brake adjustment or bearing replacement needed.
